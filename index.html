Native Iowa trees are great to consider planting in your yard. Native trees are good for the environment and help to restore Iowa's natural beauty. "Native" means that they have been occurring in this region for a long time, and can withstand the climate better than others that have been introduced to the zone. Because these trees are well adapted to the area, they are lower maintenance, preserve biodiversity, and require less water. The trees listed below are some of the fastest-growing natives that look the best in landscapes with all four seasons considered.

Quaking Aspen

Iowa's native quaking aspens bring a sparkling-leafed beauty to the state's woodlands. These slender, medium-sized trees are easily recognized by their smooth, pale greenish-white bark and their characteristic rounded leaves, which tremble in even the slightest breeze, creating a shimmering effect. They typically grow in colonies, with extensive root systems. Aspens thrive in well-drained soils and enjoy the sun. Their light shade and rapid growth provide habitat for a variety of plants and wildlife, and their presence adds a soft, rustling sound to the Iowa landscape, particularly in the northern and eastern parts of the state where they are most common.

Sugar Maple

Iowa's native sugar maples are a majestic presence in the state's woodlands, particularly in the eastern and northeastern regions. These large trees are renowned for their wild-colored fall foliage. Their leaves transform vibrantly with colors like reds, oranges, and yellows. Their smooth, gray bark and palmate leaves, typically with five pointed lobes, further contribute to their distinctive appearance. Sugar maples are adaptable trees, thriving in a variety of soil types and moisture conditions, often found in rich, well-drained soils along streams and in mixed forests. They are a keystone species in the Iowa ecosystem, providing valuable habitat and food sources for a wide range of wildlife. Their presence adds a ton of canopy shade and color in the fall.

Sycamore

Iowa's native sycamores are distinctive trees with a unique appearance.They are known for their smooth, mottled bark that peels away in large patches, revealing a lighter-colored inner bark, often referred to as “ghosts of the woods.” Their large, maple-shaped leaves provide a lot of shade, mature sycamores develop a massive, spreading crown. Sycamores are typically found along rivers and streams, thriving in moist, well-drained soils. They are fast-growing trees that can reach impressive sizes, up to 100 feet tall. This image is of a sycamore here in Ames, located on the corner of Lincoln Way and University Boulevard, known and loved by many.

Northern Hackberry

The native northern hackberry is a resilient and adaptable tree found throughout Iowa. Recognizable by its warty, gray bark and asymmetrical, toothed leaves, it thrives in a variety of conditions, from river sides to hill slopes. Hackberries are known for their ability to withstand harsh conditions, including drought and wind, making them a common tree in urban settings. They grow to a medium size, often with a broad, rounded crown, and produce small, purple berries that are a valuable food source for birds and wildlife. In the Iowa landscape, hackberries provide shade, erosion control, and wildlife habitat, blending into both natural and man-made environments due to their hardy nature and beauty.

Black Willow

The black willow is the only willow native to Iowa that grows into a larger size tree. It features dark, furrowed bark and long, slender leaves that are a vibrant green. Unlike the cultivated weeping willow, its branches tend to ascend more than droop, creating a more upright silhouette. Black willows thrive in wet environments, lining riverbanks, swamps, and pond edges, where their extensive root systems stabilize soil and prevent erosion. They grow rapidly, forming thickets and providing a great habitat for wildlife. Their presence in the Iowa landscape adds a touch of wildness to the water's edge.

Bur Oak

The Bur Oak is a majestic and sturdy tree, one of the most common oaks in Iowa. It is easily recognized by its thick, corky bark and large acorns. Bur Oaks are highly adaptable, thriving in a variety of soil types, from dry uplands to moist bottomlands. They are long-lived trees, often reaching significant sizes and ages, providing extensive shade with their broad, spreading crowns. Bur Oaks play a crucial role in Iowa's ecosystems, supporting a diverse range of wildlife with their acorns and offering habitat for birds and insects. Their deep roots help prevent soil erosion, making them a valuable species for conservation and landscape restoration.

Eastern Redbud

The eastern redbud brings a vibrant splash of color to Iowa's spring landscapes. This small tree is known for its stunning display of rosy-pink to purplish flowers that bloom directly on the branches before the leaves emerge. Redbuds prefer well-drained, slightly acidic soils and are often found in woodland edges and along streams. They typically grow with a spreading, multi-stemmed form, creating a delicate, round silhouette. Eastern redbuds serve as a delightful spring tree, brightening the landscape and providing a valuable nectar source for pollinators. Their moderate size and graceful form make them a perfect fit for smaller yards and add a touch of elegance to the woodland fringe.
